Quantum Mechanics Projects - Overview

Abstract (Overall)

This series of quantum mechanics projects aims to demystify core quantum phenomena through computational modeling and interactive visualization. By translating abstract quantum equations (Schrödinger equation, wavefunctions, tunneling) into intuitive visual outputs (plots, animations), the projects bridge the gap between theoretical quantum physics and practical understanding. The work focuses on two foundational quantum models (particle in a box, quantum tunneling) and the computational tools developed to visualize them, serving as an educational resource for students and enthusiasts new to quantum mechanics.

Project Series

Project 1: Quantum Particle in a Box

Core Intro: A visualization of the simplest quantum bound state model, exploring discrete energy levels, wavefunctions, and probability densities. Key focus: classical vs. quantum behavior in confined spaces. This project breaks down the foundational "particle in a box" model into interactive plots, making quantized energy levels tangible for learners.

Project 2: Quantum Tunneling

Core Intro: An exploration of the counterintuitive quantum effect where particles penetrate potential barriers despite insufficient energy. Includes real-world applications (STM, radioactive decay) and transmission probability calculations. This project explains why quantum particles can "tunnel" through barriers that classical particles cannot cross.

Project 1: Quantum Particle in a Box - Visualizing Wavefunctions

Abstract

This project aims to demystify the fundamental quantum mechanical model of a "particle in a one-dimensional box" through interactive visualizations. By computationally generating wavefunction plots, probability density distributions, and time-evolution animations, we bridge the gap between theoretical quantum principles and intuitive understanding. The results demonstrate the discrete energy levels of confined quantum particles and contrast classical vs. quantum behavior, providing an educational tool for students new to quantum mechanics.

Fundamental Principles

The "particle in a box" (PIB) model describes a quantum particle confined to a one-dimensional region of space (0 ≤ x ≤ L) with infinite potential barriers at the boundaries. Key principles include:

  • Boundary Conditions: The wavefunction ψ(x) = 0 at x=0 and x=L (particle cannot exist outside the box).
  • Discrete Energy Levels: Energy is quantized as Eₙ = (n²π²ℏ²)/(2mL²), where n=1,2,3... (quantum number), m=particle mass, L=box length.
  • Wavefunction Form: ψₙ(x) = √(2/L) sin(nπx/L) - describes the quantum state of the particle.
  • Probability Density: |ψₙ(x)|² - the likelihood of finding the particle at position x (contrasts with classical uniform probability).

Project 2: Quantum Tunneling - Particles Crossing Barriers

Abstract

Quantum tunneling is a counterintuitive phenomenon where a particle can penetrate a potential energy barrier despite having less energy than the barrier height—an effect impossible in classical physics. This project visualizes quantum tunneling through computational modeling, including wave packet-barrier interactions, transmission probability calculations, and real-world applications (e.g., semiconductors, radioactive decay). The interactive visualizations clarify the mathematical and physical basis of tunneling, making this advanced quantum effect accessible to learners.

Fundamental Principles

Quantum tunneling arises from the wave nature of matter and the Heisenberg uncertainty principle. Key principles include:

  • Wavefunction Penetration: A quantum particle's wavefunction ψ(x) does not drop to zero at a finite potential barrier—it decays exponentially inside the barrier and can emerge on the other side.
  • Transmission Probability: The probability of tunneling (T) depends on barrier height (V₀), barrier width (a), and particle energy (E): T ∝ e^(-2κa), where κ = √[2m(V₀-E)]/ℏ (decay constant).
  • Classical vs. Quantum Contrast: Classical particles with E < V₀ cannot cross the barrier; quantum particles have a non-zero tunneling probability (even for E << V₀).

Computational Quantum Mechanics Projects

Abstract

This coding project develops a suite of Python-based computational tools to visualize core quantum mechanical phenomena (wavefunctions, probability densities, tunneling, time evolution). The goal is to translate abstract quantum equations into interactive, visual outputs (graphs, animations) that make quantum mechanics accessible to a broader audience. The codebase is modular, well-commented, and optimized for educational use, with future plans to deploy a web-based interface (Flask/Dash) for browser-based interaction without Python installation.

Technical Stack

  • Python (core language) - chosen for readability and scientific computing libraries
  • NumPy - vectorized numerical calculations (10-100x faster than pure Python loops)
  • Matplotlib - static plots and basic animations (time evolution)
  • Seaborn - enhanced visualization styling for publication-quality plots
  • Pygame - interactive wave packet simulations (real-time user input)
