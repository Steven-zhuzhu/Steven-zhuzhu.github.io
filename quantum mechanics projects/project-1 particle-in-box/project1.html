Project 1: Quantum Particle in a Box - Visualizing Wavefunctions

Abstract

This project aims to demystify the fundamental quantum mechanical model of a "particle in a one-dimensional box" through interactive visualizations. By computationally generating wavefunction plots, probability density distributions, and time-evolution animations, we bridge the gap between theoretical quantum principles and intuitive understanding. The results demonstrate the discrete energy levels of confined quantum particles and contrast classical vs. quantum behavior, providing an educational tool for students new to quantum mechanics.

Fundamental Principles

The "particle in a box" (PIB) model describes a quantum particle confined to a one-dimensional region of space (0 ≤ x ≤ L) with infinite potential barriers at the boundaries. Key principles include:

  • Boundary Conditions: The wavefunction ψ(x) = 0 at x=0 and x=L (particle cannot exist outside the box).
  • Discrete Energy Levels: Energy is quantized as Eₙ = (n²π²ℏ²)/(2mL²), where n=1,2,3... (quantum number), m=particle mass, L=box length.
  • Wavefunction Form: ψₙ(x) = √(2/L) sin(nπx/L) - describes the quantum state of the particle.
  • Probability Density: |ψₙ(x)|² - the likelihood of finding the particle at position x (contrasts with classical uniform probability).

Unlike classical particles (which can have any energy and uniform position probability), quantum particles in a box exhibit standing wave patterns and non-uniform probability distributions—key hallmarks of quantum behavior.

Technical Implementation

Built with Python (NumPy for numerical calculations, Matplotlib for plotting, Matplotlib.animation for time evolution). The code is optimized for clarity and interactivity, making it suitable for educational purposes on a personal website. Key features include:

  • Interactive plots of wavefunctions for different energy levels (n=1 to n=5)
  • Probability density visualization showing where the particle is most likely to be found
  • Animation of wavefunction time evolution (stationary vs. superposition states)
  • Comparison of classical vs. quantum behavior in a confined space
