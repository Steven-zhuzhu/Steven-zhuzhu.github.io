Introduction of Compational Quantum Mechanics

1. Traditioanal Quantum Mechanics (TQM)

Quantum mechanics is the study of the unique physical laws governing microscopic particles such as atoms, electrons and protons. Classical physics, the set of rules we use in daily life (e.g., Newton's laws), is completely inapplicable in the microscopic world, where these tiny particles exhibit distinctive phenomena like quantum tunneling and quantum interference. Relying on precise mathematical derivation, quantum mechanics can figure out the laws of simple microscopic systems (e.g., hydrogen atoms) and calculate exact results for them. However, it has a notable limitation: once the number of microscopic particles increases (such as a system composed of dozens of atoms), the corresponding core physical equations become extremely complex, making it impossible to work out their exact solutions at all.

2. What is Computational Quantum Mechnics(CQM) Do ?

Computational quantum mechanics is created precisely to address this limitation of quantum mechanics. It does not reject the original theories of quantum mechanics; on the contrary, it strictly abides by all the rules established by quantum mechanics, only adopting a different "approach to problem-solving": it uses numerical calculation methods and high-performance computers to derive approximate solutions for complex microscopic systems. This is just like how we use computers to get practical approximate answers for complex math problems that have no exact solutions‚Äîand these results all conform to the laws of quantum mechanics.

3. What is the Relationship between CQM and TQM ?

To put it simply, quantum mechanics is the "fundamental theory" of the microscopic world, laying down the basic rules for the microscopic domain and solving simple microscopic physics problems. Computational quantum mechanics, by contrast, is a "practical tool" for real-world application: it applies the theories of quantum mechanics to complex actual microscopic systems, verifies the theoretical predictions of quantum mechanics, and discovers new quantum phenomena that cannot be observed through pure theoretical derivation alone. In this way, it enables quantum mechanics to truly solve practical microscopic physics problems in the real world.

5. Python: The Core Programming Language for CQM Research

Building on the critical role of programming in computational quantum mechanics (CQM) mentioned earlier, Python is the programming language I am most familiar with and have used most frequently for CQM work. From my practice, Python has become a top choice in the CQM field due to its simple syntax, strong readability, and rich ecosystem. It offers a wide range of professional libraries tailored for scientific computing and quantum simulation‚Äîsuch as NumPy for efficient numerical calculations, SciPy for scientific computing algorithms, and Qiskit/PySCF for quantum chemistry and quantum simulation. These libraries significantly reduce programming difficulty, allowing me and other researchers to focus more on designing quantum algorithms and analyzing simulation results, rather than writing low-level code. I also value that Python is compatible with high-performance computing frameworks like MPI, which enables it to meet the computing needs of large-scale quantum systems‚Äîstriking an excellent balance between ease of use and computational efficiency.
