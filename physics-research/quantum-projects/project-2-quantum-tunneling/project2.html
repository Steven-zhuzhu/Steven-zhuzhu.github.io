Project 2: Quantum Tunneling - Particles Crossing Barriers

Abstract

Quantum tunneling is a counterintuitive phenomenon where a particle can penetrate a potential energy barrier despite having less energy than the barrier height—an effect impossible in classical physics. This project visualizes quantum tunneling through computational modeling, including wave packet-barrier interactions, transmission probability calculations, and real-world applications (e.g., semiconductors, radioactive decay). The interactive visualizations clarify the mathematical and physical basis of tunneling, making this advanced quantum effect accessible to learners.

Fundamental Principles

Quantum tunneling arises from the wave nature of matter and the Heisenberg uncertainty principle. Key principles include:

  • Wavefunction Penetration: A quantum particle's wavefunction ψ(x) does not drop to zero at a finite potential barrier—it decays exponentially inside the barrier and can emerge on the other side.
  • Transmission Probability: The probability of tunneling (T) depends on barrier height (V₀), barrier width (a), and particle energy (E): T ∝ e^(-2κa), where κ = √[2m(V₀-E)]/ℏ (decay constant).
  • Classical vs. Quantum Contrast: Classical particles with E < V₀ cannot cross the barrier; quantum particles have a non-zero tunneling probability (even for E << V₀).
  • Uncertainty Principle: The particle's position/momentum uncertainty allows it to "borrow" energy temporarily to cross the barrier (energy-time uncertainty: ΔEΔt ≥ ℏ/2).

Tunneling is foundational to modern technology (e.g., tunnel diodes, STM microscopes) and natural processes (e.g., alpha decay, nuclear fusion in stars).

Real-Life Applications

  • Semiconductor devices (tunnel diodes, quantum tunneling transistors)
  • Radioactive decay (alpha particles tunneling out of atomic nuclei)
  • Scanning Tunneling Microscopy (STM) - a tool to image individual atoms
  • Quantum computing (tunneling in superconducting qubits)
